Article 01

Benefits of Radiation for Humans

By Senuli Rashwitha Menon · Grade 8 E

Have you ever heard the word radiation and felt a little confused or even worried? I did. The word sounded serious and even a bit dangerous. But then I started wondering—what exactly is radiation? Is it always harmful, or can it actually help us?

When I learned more, I discovered that radiation is simply energy that travels in waves or tiny particles. It is invisible, but it exists all around us. That made me think—if we cannot even see it, how does it help people?

One place where radiation helps a lot is in hospitals. Imagine a doctor trying to understand what is happening inside our bodies. Without opening the body, how could they see bones or organs? This is where machines like X-rays and CT scans help. They use radiation to create images inside the body so doctors can find broken bones, tumors, or other problems.

Radiation can also help treat diseases. In a treatment called radiation therapy, powerful rays are carefully used to destroy cancer cells. It feels almost like invisible helpers fighting illness inside the body.

Hospitals also need to keep everything very clean and safe. Radiation helps here too. It is used to sterilize medical equipment such as surgical tools, gloves, and medical supplies. By killing harmful germs, it helps protect patients from infections.

Radiation even helps with something we all care about—food. Sometimes food spoils because of bacteria. Scientists can use radiation to remove harmful bacteria and keep food fresh for longer.

Farmers also benefit from radiation. Scientists use it to develop stronger crops that grow better and resist diseases. Radiation is also used to control pests that damage crops.

Radiation helps produce electricity in nuclear power plants and helps industries check materials for hidden defects. Scientists even use it to study tiny atoms and learn more about how our world works.

So should we always be afraid of radiation? I don't think so. Like many powerful things, it depends on how we use it. When used carefully and wisely, radiation becomes a helpful tool for humans.

Now when I hear the word radiation, I no longer think only about danger. I think about something invisible but powerful that quietly helps doctors, scientists, farmers, and many others make our world healthier and safer.

Rules, Policies and Procedures on the Disposal of Nuclear Waste

By Omindie Karunawansha

The disposal of nuclear materials and radioactive elements is a highly regulated process due to the threats these waste pose on human health and the environment. Primarily, nuclear waste generators are designed to minimize waste production through proper design and operation. Waste produced must be separated into categories based on half-life and radioactivity level. Combustible waste is incinerated and solid waste is compacted to reduce the volume by up to 100 times. Then waste is sealed in specialized containers, such as steel drums, copper canisters or concrete boxes to prevent any chance of radiation exposure to humans, animals or any type of pollution.

Radioactivity of the waste decays with time, so low-level waste which are lightly contaminated can be disposed in a very straightforward manner and can be undertaken safely almost anywhere. However, high-level waste must be stored in secure containers at least for about 50 years before they can be disposed. Used fuel can have radioactive elements and these are usually stored under water for at least five years before solidifying and transferring into a dry storage facility. Most of the radioactive waste produced however are usually disposed by implementing Deep Geological Disposal. This involves placing waste 250 to 1000 m deep in stable rock formations. Other final disposal options include Near-Surface disposal (for Low-level and short-lived Intermediate-level waste) and Borehole disposal (for smaller volumes).

Safety Procedures in Nuclear Power Plants & Reactors

Research Unit

Safety conditions are performed to maintained & inspected regularly, and upgraded when necessary to ensure plants meet relevant outcomes which targets for the outcome they intended to results. Canada is home to several nuclear power plants globally. In this technology, various types of nuclear reactors were created, mainly the pressurized water reactor which is known as PWR and boiling water reactor which is known as BWR.

A pressurized water reactor uses water under high pressure, circulating within the primary circuit to remove heat from the core, whereas in a boiling reactor, heated water is boiled at a lower pressure and the resulting steam is bought directly to the turbine. Normally nuclear power plant consists of several parts which combined to perform the final output of electricity, which is produced by converting nuclear energy into heat which is then used to create steam that drives turbines connected to electric generators.

According to International Atomic Energy Emergency, Safety conditions in this type of nuclear power plant is relies on "3Cs" Control the power, Cool the fuel, Contain radioactivity using defense-in-depth barriers which composed of radioactive material is separated from the environment by multiple layers, the fuel cladding, the reactor pressure vessel, and a thick reinforced concrete containment building. Then we have automatic shutdown systems, these systems are designed to immediately stop the nuclear chain reaction if fuel temperature rises or other anomalies occur. Then we comeacross cooling systems which have passive and active systems maintain fuel cooling, even without electricity, to prevent core damage.

They are the key safety systems and procedures. Based on these three operational safety protocols have built on. They are composed of safety culture, regulatory oversight, maintenance & training and security measures. In safety culture personnel are trained to understand how their actions impact the 3Cs. Regulatory oversight which demanded based on International Atomic Energy Emergency and national regulators provide strict and regular inspections and ensuring a strong safety culture. Plants have robust procedures, including exclusion zones and emergency planning to protect workers and the public from radiation exposure. Procedures also ensure the environmental safety as it should focus on preventing the release of radioactive materials into the environment.

Article 04

World Nuclear Energy Regulatory Rules and Procedures

By Sayuri Widyarathne · B.Sc. (UG) — University of Colombo

Nuclear energy powers about 10% of the world's electricity, making robust regulation essential for safety and security.

The International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA), established in 1957, forms the backbone of global nuclear oversight. It sets international safety standards, conducts inspections, and ensures nuclear materials aren't diverted for weapons use. Over 170 countries work within its safeguards framework.

Alongside the IAEA, the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT, 1968) remains the cornerstone of international nuclear law. Other than that, individual nations maintain their own regulators which enforce strict licensing, inspection, and emergency preparedness procedures.

Therefore, at its core, nuclear regulation has one clear goal: keeping people and the planet safe, no matter how great the demand for energy.

Article 05

Natural Radiation in the Environment

By Omindie Karunawansha

Radiation is not limited to just laboratories; it is found all around us in the environment. This non-harmful radiation is known as non-ionizing radiation. It does not have sufficient energy necessary to remove electrons from atoms.

A primary source of non-ionizing radiation is found in sun light. Sun light consists of a wide spectrum of electromagnetic radiation. Even though the ozone layer blocks most of the UV rays from entering into the atmosphere, small amount of UV rays and IR rays enter.

UV rays are harmful to the human skin, however, IR rays show minimum impact on the human body. All objects emit Infrared rays as thermal radiation.

In the world of communication, microwaves and radiowaves are constantly found around us in the environment. Humans are constantly exposed to WiFi signals as well as other radiation emitted from electronic equipment such as televisions and mobile phones.

Low ionizing radiation is found in places we never think of. Trace amounts of radioactive elements are found in bricks. Even the food that we consume can contain low amount of radioactive isotopes.

Generally radioactive isotopes are harmful to humans in large amount, however the level present naturally in food is not harmful to the human body as it is found in trace amounts. Bananas can have trace amounts of radioactive potassium-40 isotopes and certain nut types can have radium.

It is necessary that we are aware of these radiation types in the natural environment.

Radiation: A Blessing in Disguise

By Omindie Karunawansha

The first thing that comes to our mind when we think about the word ‘radiation’ isn’t something pretty. Radiation is associated with many negative ideas, that it is dangerous and uncontrollable.

While these statements can be proved right to some extent by critics, radiation is very useful for mankind thanks to the recent developments in technology.

Radiation is mainly used in the medical field for radiological imaging procedures such as using X-rays for mammography, CT scans and also fluoroscopy which enables doctors to see real time motion. Radiation has become irreplaceable when it comes to radiotherapy which is used to treat cancers. Radioactive isotopes of Iodine are administered to treat thyroid gland related medical issues.

In addition to medicine, benefits of radiation span across many other fields. In food manufacturing facilities, UV rays, Gamma rays as well as X-rays are used to sterilize food, confirming food safety and preservation. Radiation had many industrial applications in the fields of aerospace and construction.

One of the most important aspects of radiation is nuclear power generation. Using a small amount of atoms and a radiation beam of electrons, a large amount of power can be generated and this is highly useful at present to address the increasing energy demand around the world.

It is high time that we embrace the benefits of radiation as a society, using it carefully and responsibly.

Nuclear Energy: From Fission to the Future

By team FLUX3

Energy is a very powerful way to make electricity. Lots of people are afraid of power because they do not understand it.. The science behind nuclear energy is very precise and controlled. It has been developed over years. This article will explain how nuclear energy works. From the process of fission to the safety systems and new ideas for the future.

The Heart of Nuclear Power: Fission

Every nuclear reactor has a process called fission at its core. This happens when an atom like Uranium-235 absorbs a neutron and becomes unstable. Then it splits into atoms and releases a lot of energy in the form of heat.

This process also releases neutrons. These neutrons can make other uranium atoms split. This creates a chain reaction that produces energy all the time.

Something important to know is that a small amount of mass is lost during fission and turned into energy. This is described by Einsteins equation E = mc². Nuclear energy uses this equation to make electricity.

Controlling the Reaction: Moderators

The neutrons that are released during fission move fast. They are too fast to make other atoms split easily. To make the reaction work well reactors use materials called moderators to slow down these neutrons.

Some common moderators are:

  • Ordinary water
  • water
  • Graphite

When these neutrons are slower they are more likely to make other atoms split. If the moderator is removed the reaction slows down or stops. This is a safety feature that is built into the reactor.

Cooling the Reactor: Heat Management

The energy that is released from fission is heat. To prevent the reactor from getting hot and damaging the fuel reactors use coolants to carry this heat away.

In reactors water does two jobs:

  • It slows down neutrons
  • It removes heat

There are types of reactors.

  • Pressurized Water Reactors use two water loops to prevent radioactive contamination.
  • Boiling Water Reactors make steam directly inside the reactor.

Cooling towers are often thought to be polluting the air.. They just release water vapor, not smoke or radiation.

Fuel and Enrichment

Natural uranium only has 0.7% Uranium-235. This is the type of uranium that is needed for fission. To make it work in reactors it must be enriched to 3-5%.

This is done using devices called gas centrifuges. They separate isotopes based on mass. The process uses uranium in the form of a gas.

Another type of uranium Uranium-238 is important because it can absorb neutrons and turn into Plutonium-239. This is another type of fuel.

Reactor Control and Nuclear Poisons

Not all atoms help the chain reaction. Some absorb neutrons without splitting. These are called poisons.

One of the important nuclear poisons is Xenon-135. It can slow down. Even stop a reactor. Engineers use substances like boric acid to control the reaction by absorbing neutrons.

Nuclear energy also uses concepts like the void coefficient to ensure safety. If coolant water turns into steam the reaction slows down of getting faster. This is another safety mechanism that is built into the reactor.

Radiation and Protection

Nuclear reactions produce three types of radiation:

  • Alpha particles
  • Beta particles
  • Gamma rays

To protect people and the environment reactors use materials like concrete. Lead for shielding.

Radiation exposure is measured in Sieverts. This shows how much it can affect living things. Workers use dosimeters to monitor their exposure.

A interesting thing that happens in reactors is the glow called Cherenkov radiation. This is caused by particles moving faster than light in water.

Advanced Safety Systems

Modern nuclear reactors are designed with safety layers.

One key feature is delayed neutrons. These give operators time to control the reaction. Without them reactors would be hard to manage.

In emergencies reactors use a system called SCRAM. This inserts control rods quickly to stop the reaction.

After shutdown reactors produce decay heat. So cooling must continue.

Newer reactors also have safety systems. These use forces like gravity and convection instead of electricity. This ensures safety during power failures.

The containment building is a concrete structure. It is designed to prevent any release of material even in extreme situations.

The Future of Nuclear Energy

technology is always improving with new innovations:

  • Small Modular Reactors are smaller and faster to deploy. They have safety features.
  • Breeder Reactors make fuel than they use. They convert Uranium-238 into Plutonium-239.
  • Thorium Reactors use Thorium-232. This is an abundant resource. It is potentially safer and more sustainable.
  • Nuclear Fusion combines atoms like hydrogen. This is how the Sun works.. It is still being developed because it is very challenging.

Balancing the Energy Trilemma

energy plays a big role in addressing the global energy trilemma. This includes:

  • Energy Security
  • Energy Equity
  • Environmental Sustainability

Today nuclear power provides about 10% of the worlds electricity. It is one of the sources of low-carbon energy.

Nuclear energy is not an uncontrollable force. It is a managed process that is based on science and engineering. From controlled chain reactions to safety systems every part of a nuclear power plant is designed with precision and protection, in mind.

The real challenge is not the technology itself. The misconceptions surrounding nuclear energy.
